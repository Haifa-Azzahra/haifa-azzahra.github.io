Emotional Intelligence for Navigating Difficult Conversations

A crash course that takes learners from theory to practice — Goleman's framework, a scenario quiz, and an AI-powered coaching simulation.

The Problem

Difficult conversations are one of the most avoided management challenges — not because managers don't care, but because they lack both the emotional vocabulary and the practice. Most EI training stops at awareness: here's what emotional intelligence is. Few programmes close the gap to here's what it looks like in a real conversation.

This course was designed to bridge that gap — moving learners from conceptual understanding to practiced skill through a progressive three-stage learning sequence.

💡 Design question: How do you give someone a safe space to practice emotionally charged conversations — without a live human on the other side?

My Design Decisions

1. Anchoring in Goleman's 4 Domains

Rather than a general overview of EI, I structured the content around Daniel Goleman's four-domain framework: Self-Awareness, Self-Management, Social Awareness, and Relationship Management. Each domain was taught through a practical application prompt tied directly to difficult conversation scenarios — not abstract definitions.

2. A Three-Stage Learning Arc

The course follows a deliberate build: theory → knowledge check → applied simulation. This mirrors the spaced practice principle and ensures learners aren't dumped into a simulation cold. Each stage activates and builds on the previous one.

  • Stage 1 — Conceptual: Goleman's framework explained with practical use-it prompts per domain.
  • Stage 2 — Knowledge Check: 5 scenario-based single-choice questions, with immediate feedback and correct answer explanations after each response.
  • Stage 3 — AI Simulation: Learners take the role of Raine's manager in a live, branching conversation built with Claude. A simulation disclaimer and structured debrief are included.

3. The AI Simulation — Rationale and Boundaries

The simulation is the course's centrepiece — and its most deliberate design decision. I chose to use an AI-powered simulation (rather than a branching scenario with pre-written branches) because it allows open-ended, natural conversation practice. Learners can't predict or click through; they have to actually respond.

To manage the risk of learners misreading the simulation as a substitute for real human interaction, I wrote a simulation disclaimer adapted from the "pilot and flight simulator" metaphor: essential preparation, not the real thing. This framing sets accurate expectations and reinforces the psychological safety the simulation is designed to create.

📌 Key takeaway embedded in the debrief: "Impact matters more than intent" — a principle that surfaces through the scenario, not just told.

4. Emotional Safety by Design

The scenario (Raine) was written so there's no clear villain. Raine believes the HR concern from six months ago was settled. Her manager must reopen it without damaging the relationship. This moral ambiguity forces learners to practice empathy-under-pressure — the hardest EI skill to develop in a low-stakes environment.

The Outcome

The module closes the loop: learners end with both the Goleman framework and a simulated coaching conversation in their lived experience. The debrief section translates the simulation back into transferable principles — so what happened with Raine becomes a mental model for the next real conversation.

This is what learning transfer looks like in practice: not a test of content recall, but a rehearsal of the behaviour you want to see on the job.
